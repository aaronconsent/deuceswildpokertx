The One Chart to Memorize

Hand Rankings, Best to Worst

Every poker hand is five cards. When the betting is done, the best five-card hand wins. Higher on this list beats lower — that's the whole game.

#HandWhat it is
1 Royal Flush 10-J-Q-K-A, all the same suit. The best possible hand. You'll go your whole life rarely seeing one.
2 Straight Flush Five cards in a row, all the same suit (like 5-6-7-8-9 of clubs).
3 Four of a Kind All four cards of one rank — four Kings, for example.
4 Full House Three of a kind plus a pair (three 8s and two 4s).
5 Flush Five cards of the same suit, in any order.
6 Straight Five cards in a row of mixed suits (like 6-7-8-9-10).
7 Three of a Kind Three cards of the same rank.
8 Two Pair Two separate pairs (two Jacks and two 5s).
9 One Pair Two cards of the same rank. Surprisingly often enough to win.
10 High Card No pair, no anything — your single highest card decides it.

Tip: there's a printed copy at every table, and the dealer will happily confirm who wins. Nobody memorizes this on day one.

It's Simpler Than It Looks

How a Hand Actually Plays

One full hand of Texas Hold'em, start to finish.

1. Blinds & your two cards

Two players post forced bets called the blinds so there's a pot to play for. Then the dealer gives you two private cards, face down. Those two are yours alone — keep them hidden.

2. The shared cards

The dealer lays three cards face up in the middle (the flop), then one more (the turn), then a final one (the river). Everyone shares these. You make your best five-card hand from your two cards plus these five.

3. Bet, or get out

Between each step there's a round of betting. On your turn you can fold (toss your cards, you're out of this hand), call (match the bet), or raise (bet more). Folding when you've got nothing is completely normal — pros fold most hands.

The showdown. If two or more players are still in after the river, everyone left turns their cards face up and the best hand wins the pot. If everyone else folded along the way, the last player standing scoops the pot without ever showing their cards. New hand, repeat.

Demystifying the Table

What You'll See When You Sit Down

The chips

You trade cash for chips when you sit. Each color is a different value — the dealer will tell you which is which. You bet by sliding chips into the middle. That's all chips are: a count of your money on the table.

The dealer

A professional dealer runs every hand: shuffles, deals, manages the pot, and announces whose turn it is. They are there to help you. Truly — ask them anything.

The button & the blinds

A small disc called the "button" marks the dealer position and moves one seat to the left each hand. The two players to its left post the small and big blinds. It rotates so everyone pays their fair share over time.

How the action moves

Betting goes around the table one player at a time, clockwise. When it's your turn, you act; otherwise you just watch and wait. No rush — take the second you need to decide.

Relax — You've Got This

The 5 Things to Know Your First Night

1. You can just watch first

Pull up near a table and watch a few hands before you sit. Nobody minds. It's the fastest way to get comfortable.

2. Ask the dealer anything

"Is it my turn?" "Did I win?" "How much can I bet?" — all fair game. The dealer is there to help and won't judge you.

3. Start small

Jump in with the $60 tournament or sit in a small cash game. You set how much you put on the table — there's a comfortable starting point for everyone.

4. Nobody expects you to be good

Everyone at the table started where you are. Make a mistake, laugh it off, keep playing. That's the whole point of a friendly room.

5. Bring ID and cash

Have a photo ID and cash ready when you walk in. No reservation, no membership — just come on a night we're open and we'll get you a seat.
