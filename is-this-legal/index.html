Is private poker legal in Texas?

Texas law includes a private-place exemption in Penal Code §47.02. A private poker club like Deuces Wild operates under that exemption — players play against each other, and the house never takes a cut of the pot.

Texas treats gambling differently depending on who profits. The problem the law targets is a house that runs a game for profit — taking a percentage of every pot, booking bets, or otherwise having a financial stake in who wins. When the people playing are simply competing against each other for their own money, and nobody behind the counter is skimming the pot, the law provides an exemption. That's the lane we operate in.

Plain English: the law says a host can't profit from the game itself. We don't. That single fact — no rake — is the backbone of how we stay on the right side of the line.

This page explains how our club operates. It is a description of our practices, not legal advice. If you want a legal opinion about your own situation, talk to a Texas attorney.

What is the §47.02 private-place exemption?

It's the part of Texas law that allows a game to be played in a private place as long as no one but the players profits and everyone faces the same odds.

In plain terms, the exemption generally turns on three conditions, and we build the whole club around meeting them:

Private Place

The game takes place in a private setting — a members' club, not a public commercial gambling hall open to anyone walking off the street.

No House Profit

No one receives any economic benefit from the game other than their own personal winnings. The house takes no rake and has no stake in any pot.

Equal Odds

The chances of winning and losing are the same for every player. Nobody at the table — including the club — gets a built-in edge.

When all three hold, the game falls within the private-place exemption. The single condition that trips up shady operations is the second one — and it's the one we never compromise on.

What does rake-free mean legally?

Rake-free means the house never takes a percentage or cut of any pot. You win and lose only against the other players — never against us.

At a typical commercial card room, the house takes a "rake" — a slice of every pot, often a few dollars a hand. That's the house profiting from the game, and it's exactly what the private-place exemption does not allow. So we don't do it. Not on cash games, not on tournaments. Ever.

Any fees at Deuces Wild are optional and flat — for example a $20 optional Last Longer or a $5 optional High Hand in a tournament. Those are never a share of the money on the table, and you can skip them entirely. This is the financial heart of how we operate: the money you put in play stays between you and the other players.

This is why rake-free isn't just a perk — it's the whole point. It's better for your bankroll and it's the legal foundation the club is built on.
